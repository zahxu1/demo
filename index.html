01 — The Problem

Why this matters.

According to Singapore's National Registry of Diseases, the incidence rate of unintentional falls among adults over 60 is 277.7 per 100,000 — and many of these happen at home, often alone. When no one is nearby, even a minor fall can become life-threatening.

Existing solutions fall short. Smart home systems require WiFi. Emergency buttons need to be physically reached. Smartphones assume the user is mobile and tech-literate.

This project takes a different approach: a passive, always-on device designed to sit near a bathroom — one of the highest-risk areas for elderly falls. No button to press. No app to open. Just speak, and it responds.

The phrase "Help me now" was chosen deliberately — three syllables, easy to distinguish from background speech, and naturally translatable across Singapore's three main languages: English, Mandarin, and Malay.

03 — Technology

What is TinyML?

TinyML is the practice of running artificial intelligence and machine learning models directly on tiny, low-power microcontrollers — the same kind of chips found in everyday devices like smartwatches and hearing aids.

Traditional AI requires a powerful computer or internet connection to process data in the cloud. TinyML flips that model: the entire "thinking" happens on the device itself, in real time, with no Wi-Fi needed.

For a distress detection device, this matters enormously. A bathroom emergency can't wait for a cloud server to respond. The device needs to react in under a second, even if the router is down.

The tradeoff is that the AI model must be compressed to fit inside just a few kilobytes of memory — roughly 10,000× smaller than a typical smartphone app. Every design decision in this project had to account for that constraint.

04 — Tools

What is Edge Impulse?

Edge Impulse is the platform used to build, train, and export the AI model in this project. Think of it as the "workshop" where raw audio recordings get turned into a working AI classifier small enough to fit on a microcontroller.

Without Edge Impulse, building a TinyML model would require writing thousands of lines of signal processing code from scratch. The platform handles the hard parts — data labelling, feature extraction, neural network training, and model optimisation — through a visual interface.

For this project, Edge Impulse was used to label 5,710 audio samples, extract MFCC audio features, train a 1D Convolutional Neural Network, and export the final model as a C++ library ready to flash onto the Arduino.

🎙

Data collection

Audio was recorded directly through the Arduino's built-in microphone and uploaded to Edge Impulse, labelled by phrase and language.

📊

MFCC feature extraction

Raw audio is too large for a microcontroller. MFCC (Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients) converts it into a compact fingerprint that captures the key characteristics of human speech.

🧠

Model training

Edge Impulse trained a neural network to classify 5 categories: "Help me now", "Jiù Míng", "Tolong saya", background noise, and unknown speech.

📦

One-click deployment

The trained model was exported as an Arduino-compatible C++ library and flashed directly to the board — no manual conversion needed.

06 — The Process

How it was built.

Built entirely from scratch — from raw audio recording to on-device deployment. Every stage involved real decisions, real failures, and real iteration.

01 🎙

Dataset collection

Personally recorded 5,710 labelled audio samples across 30 different voices — varying age, gender, distance, and tone. Bathroom sounds like running taps and flushing were deliberately included so the model learns to ignore them.

02 ⚙️

Feature engineering

Used Edge Impulse's MFCC block to convert raw audio into compact spectral features — stripping redundant data and compressing each sample into a small fingerprint the microcontroller can process instantly.

03 🧠

Model training

Trained a 1D Convolutional Neural Network over 200 cycles at a 0.005 learning rate — classifying 5 categories: the 3 phrases, background noise, and unknown speech. Final accuracy: 90.59%.

04 🔌

Deployment & casing

Exported as a C++ Arduino library, flashed to the board, and wired to three LEDs with resistors — all housed in a custom 3D printed enclosure running off a 9V battery. No computer, no cloud.

07 — Reflection

What I learned & what's next.

The biggest lesson was that data collection method matters more than model complexity. My first attempt used phone-recorded audio — convenient, but the microphone characteristics didn't match the Arduino's built-in mic, causing frequent false positives when deployed. Switching to the board's own microphone made a significant difference.

Including bathroom noise samples — tap water, toilet flushing, water droplets — turned out to be one of the most important decisions. Without them, the model couldn't reliably filter out the very environment it's deployed in.

Collecting data from 30 different voices, spanning different ages, genders, and speaking distances, was tedious but essential. A model trained only on your own voice will fail in the real world.
