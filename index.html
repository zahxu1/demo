01 — The Problem

Why this project exists.

Singapore's National Registry of Diseases records 277.7 falls per 100,000 adults over 60 every year. Most happen at home — often when no one else is present. A fall that goes undetected for hours can be fatal.

Current solutions each have a critical gap. Panic buttons require the user to physically reach and press them during an emergency. Wearables only work if worn. Smartphones depend on dexterity, awareness, and battery charge. None of these are guaranteed when someone is in genuine distress.

This project investigates how TinyML and edge AI can be used to create a lightweight, low-power embedded system capable of continuously detecting emergency distress phrases fully offline in noisy bathroom environments..

The phrase "Help me now" was chosen because its three syllables make it far easier for a neural network to distinguish from normal conversation than single-syllable words, which are prone to false positives. It was also chosen because it translates directly into the three main languages spoken by Singapore's elderly population.

03 — Core Technology

What is TinyML?

TinyML represents a fascinating field of study that marries machine learning with embedded systems. Its primary aim is to investigate the feasibility of running models on small, energy-efficient device such as microcontrollers. This technology open doors to achieving low latency, energy-efficient, and bandwidth-frugal model inferences directly at the edge, where data is generated.

TinyML offers various educational opportunities, including courses and programs, to delve into its intricacies. The applications of TinyML extend across a wide spectrum of industries, with a pronounced impact on sectors reliant on the Internet of Things (IoT) networks and data.

A keyword spotting system is a form of speech recognition technology meticulously designed to identify specific keywords or phrases within spoken language. This technology is prevalent in voice-activated applications and devices, such as virtual assistants, smart speakers, and various IoT devices. In real-time, the system analyses audio input, actively searching for predefined keywords or phrases set by developers or users. Upon detection, the system executes predetermined actions or provides responses based on programmed instructions. The widespread adoption of this technology spans across diverse industries, including healthcare, automotive, and retail, enhancing user experiences by delivering more intuitive and personalised interactions. .

04 — Development Platform

What is Edge Impulse?

Edge Impulse is the platform used to build and train the AI model. It takes raw audio recordings and turns them into a finished C++ library small enough to run on a microcontroller — handling signal processing, neural network training, and hardware export automatically.

Edge Impulse stands as a versatile platform meticulously crafted to address the requirements of developers aiming to deploy embedded machine-learning solutions at scale. This seamlessly integrated platform amalgamates key components such as software-as-a-service, developer tools, embedded software, and comprehensive documentation. Its design is dedicated to empowering embedded development teams, providing the necessary tools and resources to successfully leverage machine learning within an embedded context.

One approach to designing an impulse involves utilizing a data set, wherein the raw data is segmented into smaller windows and processed using signal processing blocks to extract key features. The resulting data is then classified using a learning block. Signal processing blocks are useful for making raw data easier to handle, and learning blocks can learn from past experiences. For the current project, as the device is intended to recognize human speech, it is recommended to utilize the MFCC block. This block leverages Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients to simplify raw audio data, which typically contains a significant amount of redundant information. The "MFCC" block is particularly effective for processing human speech.

For this project, 5,710 audio clips were recorded directly through the Arduino's own microphone and fed into Edge Impulse. The platform extracted MFCC features, trained the classifier, and packaged the final model as a plug-in Arduino library — ready to flash with one click.

06 — How It Was Built

From data collection to deployment.

Every stage was done from scratch — no pre-trained model, no existing dataset, no off-the-shelf solution. What follows is a direct account of what each stage involved and what was learned along the way.

Step 01 🎙

Building the dataset

5,710 audio clips were recorded across 30 contributors — deliberately varied by age, gender, distance from the mic, and speaking style. Bathroom sounds (tap water, flushing, water droplets) were recorded separately as negative examples. When it comes to creating a robust machine learning model, collecting more data is crucial. In fact, the more data you have, the better the model will be at learning patterns in data sets. It's important to train the model with various voices saying the phrases to ensure accuracy.

Step 02 📊

Signal processing

Each 1-second audio clip was processed through Edge Impulse's MFCC block, converting raw waveforms into compact spectral fingerprints. Raw audio is far too large for a microcontroller — MFCC extracts only the frequency characteristics that distinguish human speech, discarding everything else. The feature explorer was used to visually inspect the dataset and confirm the five classes were separating cleanly before training began.

Step 03 🧠

Training the model

A 1D Convolutional Neural Network was configured with four intermediate layers, trained over 200 cycles at a 0.005 learning rate. The dataset was split into a training set (5,710 samples) and a separate test set (894 samples) — the model never saw the test data during training. Final accuracy on the unseen test set: 90.59%. The quantised (int8) version was selected for deployment: 13KB flash, 299ms total latency.

Step 04 🔌

Deployment and enclosure

The trained model was exported as an Arduino C++ library and integrated into the firmware with custom LED logic — each phrase mapped to a specific pin and colour. The circuit uses 1x 68Ω and 2x 50Ω resistors to protect the LEDs, powered by a 9V battery. The entire assembly was mounted inside a custom 3D printed enclosure with cutouts for the LED indicators and microphone.

07 — Reflection

What I learned and what's next.

major takeaway was the importance of designing systems around real deployment conditions. Including realistic bathroom sounds such as running taps, flushing, water splashes, and background conversations became essential for reducing false positives and improving robustness during live inferencing..

Beyond machine learning, the project strengthened my understanding of embedded systems, signal processing, hardware-software integration, and balancing engineering trade-offs such as memory usage, latency, power consumption, and model performance..

Collecting 30 different voices was time-consuming but non-negotiable. A model trained on one or two voices will overfit to those specific vocal characteristics and fail on anyone new. The testing results — 5 out of 7 first-attempt detections across unfamiliar voices — confirmed that voice diversity in the dataset directly produces robustness in the field.

I also learned the importance of resilience and independent problem-solving throughout development. Many stages of the project involved failed experiments, inaccurate predictions, unstable deployments, and repeated retraining cycles before improvements were achieved. Troubleshooting these issues independently helped me become more methodical in analysing system failures and refining solutions iteratively.
